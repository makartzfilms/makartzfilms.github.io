Kelly Louton and Joshua Aucoin don't finish each other's sentences, but they don't need to. Sitting across from each other in a post-production suite in Brooklyn, the co-directors of *Domino* have the easy shorthand of collaborators who've been through something together — the kind of rapport that only forms when two people have spent months making hundreds of small, consequential decisions under pressure.

**How did the project first come together?**

**Kelly Louton:** Josh and I had been circling a collaboration for a while. We'd met through mutual friends in the New York indie scene and kept having these long conversations about family dynamics in film — how most movies either sentimentalize families or pathologize them, and how rarely you see something that captures the actual texture of how families operate. When Jacob Schwartz brought us the story concept, it felt like the conversation we'd been having for years had suddenly found its vehicle.

**Joshua Aucoin:** The script came together fast, which almost never happens. Jacob had this core idea about silence as a family's operating system — the way certain families maintain stability by never naming the thing that's wrong. Once we had that, the scenes started writing themselves. The challenge was structural: how do you build tension in a story where the central conflict is something nobody talks about?

**The film has a very specific visual grammar. How did you arrive at that?**

**KL:** Early on, we made a rule: the camera could only be as close to a character as that character was willing to be honest. So in the first act, when everyone's maintaining the facade, you get these wider, more composed shots. As the cracks start to show, the camera moves in. By the third act, we're handheld, tight on faces. It's not a gimmick — it's a way of letting the visual language do the emotional work that the dialogue can't.

**JA:** Our DP understood it immediately. We showed her about thirty reference images — mostly from Varda and Cassavetes — and she came back with a shot list that was better than what we'd imagined. That's the thing about collaboration: when the right people understand the intent, they push it further than you could alone.

**What was the hardest scene to shoot?**

**KL:** The dinner scene in the second act. It's seven pages of dialogue with five characters, and it has to feel completely naturalistic while also being the structural hinge of the entire film. We rehearsed it for three days before we shot it, and even then, it took eleven takes to get the version that's in the film. Take eleven was the one where something real happened between the actors that none of us had planned.

**JA:** I'd add the final scene, but for different reasons. It's technically simple — two people, one room, natural light. But emotionally, it's carrying the weight of everything that came before it. The actors had to be completely present, and we had to be willing to let the camera run longer than felt comfortable. The final cut of that scene is almost four minutes of unbroken silence and gesture. That took trust.

**What do you want audiences to take away?**

**JA:** I want them to recognize something. Not the specific family in the film, but the dynamic — the way love and avoidance can coexist in the same household for years. I think most people know that feeling, even if they've never articulated it. If the film gives them language for it, that's enough.

**KL:** I'd say the same thing differently. I want people to leave the theater and call someone. Not because the film is sentimental — it's not — but because it makes you think about what you're not saying to the people closest to you, and whether that silence is protecting anyone or just preserving a comfortable lie.
