Development is the least glamorous word in independent film, which is ironic because it's where every good film actually begins. When people outside the industry hear "development," they picture writers' rooms and pitch meetings — the Hollywood version, where ideas get workshopped in rooms with catered lunches. At an independent agency, development looks like a stack of scripts on a kitchen table, a lot of difficult phone calls, and the willingness to tell a filmmaker that the thing they love most about their script is the thing that's preventing it from getting made.

That last part is the hardest. Every development executive will tell you that giving notes is an art, but at the independent level, it's more specific than that. It's the art of honesty without destruction. When a filmmaker brings us a script, they're bringing something personal — a story they've lived with for months or years, shaped by their own obsessions and blind spots. Our job isn't to fix it. Our job is to help them see it clearly enough to fix it themselves.

The distinction between a good script and a producible script is one that most emerging filmmakers haven't encountered yet. A good script has voice, vision, and emotional truth. A producible script has all of that plus a realistic relationship to budget, schedule, and market. Those two things aren't in tension — or they shouldn't be — but reconciling them requires a kind of creative pragmatism that doesn't come naturally to most artists. That's where we come in.

Here's what a typical development process looks like at MakArtz. We read the script cold. No pitch, no lookbook, no sizzle reel — just the pages. If the voice is there, we schedule a conversation. That first call isn't about notes. It's about understanding what the filmmaker is trying to do, what they're afraid of, and what they're unwilling to compromise on. Those three things tell us everything we need to know about whether this is a project we can champion.

If we decide to move forward, we do a full development pass. That means a detailed written response — not coverage, not a scorecard, but a letter to the filmmaker that engages with the script on its own terms. We identify what's working, what's not, and — most importantly — why. Then we get on the phone and talk it through. Sometimes that conversation takes an hour. Sometimes it takes three. The filmmaker goes away, does a revision, and we do it again. Most projects go through three to five rounds before we consider them ready for packaging.

The thing that separates good development from bad development is patience. Bad development is reactive — it chases trends, imposes formulas, and optimizes for a market that will have moved on by the time the film is finished. Good development trusts the filmmaker's instincts while holding them accountable to their own ambitions. It takes longer. It's harder to quantify. And it's the reason our films sound like the people who made them, not like the agency that produced them.
