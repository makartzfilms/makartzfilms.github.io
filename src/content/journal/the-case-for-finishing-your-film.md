There's a specific kind of panic that sets in about three months after principal photography wraps. The festival deadlines start appearing on the calendar. The financiers start asking for rough cuts. Your sales agent — if you have one — wants to know when they can start showing the film. And suddenly, post-production stops being a creative process and becomes a countdown.

I've watched this play out dozens of times, and the result is almost always the same: the filmmaker rushes to lock a cut that isn't ready, submits to festivals with a mix that hasn't been finalized, and either gets rejected or — worse — gets in with a version of the film that doesn't represent their vision. Both outcomes are preventable. Both come from the same mistake: treating "finished" as a deadline rather than a standard.

Here's what "finished" actually means. It means the sound mix has been done in a proper room by someone who's mixed for theatrical exhibition, not just your editor's headphones. It means the color grade isn't a LUT slapped on in DaVinci but a deliberate, scene-by-scene conversation between the colorist and the director of photography. It means the music rights are cleared, the E&O insurance is in place, and the deliverables package is complete. It means a festival programmer can watch the film and know, without question, that this is the version the filmmaker intended.

The practical argument for finishing early is straightforward: festivals don't give you credit for potential. They judge what's on screen. A film that's 90% there will lose to a film that's 100% there, every time. And in a marketplace where acquisition offers are getting smaller and more conditional, showing up with an unfinished film signals that you don't have the resources or the discipline to deliver — even if neither is true.

The creative argument is more important. Post-production is where films find their rhythm. It's where happy accidents in the edit become defining structural choices. It's where a scene you thought was the heart of the film reveals itself as expendable, and a throwaway moment becomes the emotional anchor. That process takes time, and it can't be rushed without cost.

My advice to every filmmaker we work with is the same: build your post-production timeline backward from your ideal premiere date, add two months, and then defend that timeline against every pressure to compress it. Your film will be better. Your festival prospects will be better. And you'll arrive at your premiere knowing that the thing on screen is exactly what you meant to make.
