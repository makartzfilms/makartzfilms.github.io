People ask me this constantly. MakArtz Films has built its reputation on character-driven dramas and quiet, observational documentaries. So when word got out that we were producing a horror film, the reaction from our corner of the industry was — let's call it curious. A few people were blunt: "Since when do you do horror?"

The honest answer is: we don't do horror. We do films where the filmmaker has something specific to say and the craft to say it. Toy Chest happened to be one of those films. That it also happens to be terrifying is a feature, not a compromise.

The script landed on my desk through an unusual route — a cold submission, which almost never works but occasionally produces something that rewires your assumptions. The first ten pages read like a domestic drama. A mother and daughter in a house that's too quiet, navigating grief with the careful choreography of people who've agreed not to fall apart. It was restrained, specific, and deeply felt. Then the toy chest opened, and I understood what kind of film this was.

What made it a MakArtz project wasn't the genre. It was the specificity. The writer understood that horror works best when the audience cares about the people being terrorized — and that caring requires the same attention to character, dialogue, and emotional truth that you'd bring to any drama. The supernatural elements in Toy Chest aren't metaphors bolted onto a genre framework. They're extensions of a grief that the characters can't process through normal channels. The horror isn't separate from the emotional story. It is the emotional story.

Production was unlike anything we'd done before. Horror has its own technical vocabulary — lighting setups that take hours, sound design that starts in pre-production rather than post, practical effects that require rehearsal and precision. Our team had to learn a new craft language while maintaining the character-first approach that defines everything we produce. It was uncomfortable in the best way.

The result is a film that doesn't fit neatly into any programming slot, which is exactly where we like to operate. It's too emotionally grounded for horror purists and too genuinely frightening for the arthouse crowd. We think that's a strength. The films that last — the ones people are still talking about a decade later — are almost always the ones that refused to choose a lane.

We said yes to Toy Chest because it reminded us why we started this company: to champion films that couldn't exist without advocates who believe in them completely. Genre is irrelevant. Vision is everything.
